# Roll for Brew: Sourcing the Illustrated Tavern Backdrop (Issue #82 / #83)

**Date:** 2026-07-22
**Context:** Issue #64 (closed) shipped the gold card-frame Room UI (`CardFrame`/`PlayerTile`, `tavern`/`gilt`/`parchment`/`ember` Tailwind tokens) over a tiled pixel-art wood-plank texture, explicitly called out as a placeholder. Issue #82 wants that texture replaced with a fuller illustrated pixel-art tavern scene (kettles, teapots, cups, sugar cubes) that still tiles/full-bleeds the same way. This document answers the sourcing question raised in issue #83.
**Scale of the ask:** One full-bleed background illustration, not a tileset or sprite sheet. Solo maintainer, hobby budget, "nice to have polish," current placeholder is already shippable.

---

## Route 1: Commission a human pixel artist

- **Where solo/hobby devs actually find one:**
  - itch.io's "Help Wanted or Offered" forum is the standard venue — a running stream of `[FOR HIRE] [ART]` posts from pixel artists advertising availability, portfolio, and rates, e.g. [Pixel artist looking for paid projects or commissions](https://itch.io/t/6617627/for-hire-art-pixel-artist-looking-for-paid-projects-or-commissions), [Pixel art commissions for video games](https://itch.io/t/5061262/artfor-hire-pixel-art-commissions-for-video-games). Individual artists also post dedicated commission-info pages as itch.io "asset pack" listings with prices baked in, e.g. [Pixel Art Commission](https://pixeljad.itch.io/pixelartcommission/purchase).
  - Fiverr's dedicated category: [Pixel game art design services](https://www.fiverr.com/categories/graphics-design/buy/game-art/pixel) lists individual freelancer gigs with fixed starting prices, visible portfolios, and delivery-time commitments up front — the most "checkout button" version of this route.
  - Reddit's r/PixelArt commission threads and Twitter/X pixel-art community exist but (unlike itch.io/Fiverr) don't surface stable, citable primary-source pricing pages — postings are ephemeral and rates are negotiated in DMs, so treat this as a sourcing channel to browse for style fit, not a place to get a quotable price without direct contact.
- **Typical price for a single background/scene illustration (not a tileset/sprite sheet):**
  - Fiverr gig listings for pixel-art backgrounds/scenes: [$15](https://www.fiverr.com/iceebit/crate-pixel-art-character-for-you), [$25](https://www.fiverr.com/falahzaidan511/draw-you-professional-pixel-art-scene-and-background), [$30](https://www.fiverr.com/blackoutstar/create-a-cozy-pixel-art-background), up to [$90](https://www.fiverr.com/a_moneer/make-backgrounds-and-landscape) for more detailed/animated background work. These are starting ("basic tier") prices — a fuller, more detailed scene (multiple props, more colors, revisions) typically sits above the gig's advertised floor once you factor in Fiverr's package upsells.
  - itch.io-side commission listings skew both cheaper and pricier depending on experience/detail: some artists price per-piece from roughly $10-70 for simpler work, while more illustrative "fully rendered background/scene" commissions were seen quoted from roughly €160+ for a detailed painted-pixel scene (per itch.io commission-info postings surfaced in search).
  - Net range for a single detailed tavern-scene illustration from a competent pixel artist: **roughly $25-150**, with $150+ possible for an artist with a strong portfolio doing multiple revision rounds; budget freelancers on Fiverr can come in under $30 but with less room for back-and-forth.
- **Typical turnaround:** Fiverr gigs commonly advertise delivery windows of a few days to ~1-2 weeks for a single illustration (varies per seller, shown on each gig page). itch.io freelancers negotiate turnaround directly; simple single-scene commissions are generally days-to-two-weeks, not months, since it's one piece rather than a full asset set.
- **Effort for the maintainer:** Low-to-medium. Browsing/vetting a few portfolios, writing a clear brief (scene composition, palette matching the app's `tavern`/`gilt`/`parchment`/`ember` tokens, must-tile-full-bleed requirement stated explicitly), then a short review/revision loop. Commercial usage rights should be confirmed explicitly in the commission agreement — Fiverr/itch commission norms generally transfer usage rights for commercial games/apps once paid, but this varies per seller and is worth stating up front rather than assuming.

Sources: [itch.io Help Wanted or Offered forum](https://itch.io/t/6617627/for-hire-art-pixel-artist-looking-for-paid-projects-or-commissions), [Fiverr pixel game art category](https://www.fiverr.com/categories/graphics-design/buy/game-art/pixel), individual Fiverr gig listings linked inline above, [pixeljad commission page](https://pixeljad.itch.io/pixelartcommission/purchase).

## Route 2: AI image generation

The core problem this route has to solve: most general-purpose diffusion models (Midjourney, DALL-E, base Stable Diffusion/Flux) were trained overwhelmingly on photos and digital paintings, so an unassisted "pixel art tavern" prompt tends to produce a photorealistic or painterly image with a pixelation filter over it — soft anti-aliased edges, gradient-heavy shading, and no real grid alignment or palette discipline. Getting *genuine* pixel art (hard edges, a limited palette, pixels aligned to a low-resolution grid, then optionally upscaled with nearest-neighbor) requires either a model specifically trained/fine-tuned for it, or a dedicated pixel-art tool built around that constraint.

- **Dedicated pixel-art generation tools (purpose-built, not general models with a filter):**
  - **[Retro Diffusion](https://retrodiffusion.ai/)** — an AI image generator built specifically around genuine pixel-art output; also ships as a paid [Aseprite extension](https://astropulse.itch.io/retrodiffusion) ($65 one-time, or a $20 "Lite" tier). The web app uses prepaid, non-expiring credits at roughly $0.01/credit, with 50 free starter credits for new accounts; a static image up to 276×276px costs ~1 credit, larger sizes cost more (source: search of retrodiffusion.ai pricing/FAQ content, corroborated by the [Aseprite extension listing](https://astropulse.itch.io/retrodiffusion) and [Retro Diffusion Credits](https://astropulse.gumroad.com/l/RetroDiffusionCredits) Gumroad page). This is the most-cited "actually looks like pixel art" AI tool in the pixel-art dev community.
  - **[PixelLab](https://www.pixellab.ai/pixellab-api)** — an API/app purpose-built for game-dev pixel art: text-to-pixel-art, image-to-pixel-art, tileset and character/animation generation, with explicit support for "init images and forced palettes" and palette-preserving resize — i.e. it's engineered around genuine palette/grid constraints rather than a post-hoc filter. Pricing is per-generation credits, e.g. a 64×64 image costs roughly $0.007-0.008, with "Pro" tools for larger canvases (up to 512×512) at roughly $0.095-0.185 per generation. A single full-bleed background at a larger canvas size would land toward the higher end of that per-generation range, still well under a few dollars for the image itself (source: [pixellab.ai/pixellab-api](https://www.pixellab.ai/pixellab-api)).
  - **Stable Diffusion + a pixel-art LoRA** — the DIY route: run SD 1.5 or SDXL with a community-trained pixel-art LoRA layered on top. [Pixel Art XL](https://civitai.com/models/120096/pixel-art-xl) (100k+ downloads on Civitai) is the most popular SDXL option; [8bitdiffuser 64x](https://civitai.com/models/185743/8bitdiffuser-64x-or-a-perfect-pixel-art-model) and several SD1.5 "Pixel-art" LoRAs are other well-used options. This route gives the most control (palette, resolution, style-matching) but requires either local GPU hardware or a paid hosted-inference service (e.g. Civitai's own generation credits, or Colab/RunPod), plus real prompt-craft experience with LoRA weights, trigger words, and post-processing (palette quantization, nearest-neighbor scaling) to get a clean, non-muddy result.
- **Prompt-iteration effort for a non-artist:** With a dedicated tool (Retro Diffusion or PixelLab), realistic expectations are a handful of prompt iterations (5-20 generations) to land on an acceptable composition and palette, at fractions of a cent to a few cents each — cheap enough to brute-force iterate. The harder part isn't the pixel-art fidelity (the tools solve that) but composition control: getting "kettles, teapots, cups, sugar cubes" specifically arranged as a coherent full-bleed scene that reads well behind UI cards, rather than a generic tavern. Expect to also need a manual tiling/edge-blending pass afterward (in Aseprite, Photopea, or similar) since these tools generate a single image, not a guaranteed-seamless tile — the existing wood-plank placeholder presumably already solved edge-tiling once and that lesson would need reapplying. The bare Stable Diffusion + LoRA DIY route is materially more effort for a non-artist (installing/configuring a pipeline or renting GPU time, tuning LoRA weight, fixing muddy/off-grid output) and is not the fastest path here.

Sources: [Retro Diffusion](https://retrodiffusion.ai/), [Retro Diffusion Aseprite extension](https://astropulse.itch.io/retrodiffusion), [PixelLab API](https://www.pixellab.ai/pixellab-api), [Pixel Art XL LoRA](https://civitai.com/models/120096/pixel-art-xl), [8bitdiffuser 64x LoRA](https://civitai.com/models/185743/8bitdiffuser-64x-or-a-perfect-pixel-art-model).

## Route 3: Licensed asset packs (itch.io / OpenGameArt)

Searched both sites for tavern-scene, wood-plank, and tea/coffee-adjacent pixel art. Findings, with actual license text checked per listing (not assumed):

- **[Tavern background](https://opengameart.org/content/tavern-background) (OpenGameArt, by Nila122)** — full static tavern-interior background image, PNG at 800×600 / 1024×768 / 1600×1200. **License: CC-BY-SA 3.0** — commercial use is explicitly permitted, but requires (a) attribution to the artist and (b) that derivative works are shared under the same CC-BY-SA license. That share-alike clause is the catch: if this background were adapted (recolored, cropped, edited) and shipped, CC-BY-SA's copyleft would technically require releasing the derivative under the same license — worth a deliberate decision, not a rubber-stamp, before use in a product that might carry ads/subscriptions. Also: it's a painted illustration, not pixel art, and not a tileable pattern — doesn't fit the pixel-art full-bleed-tile requirement as-is.
- **[[LPC] Tavern](https://opengameart.org/content/lpc-tavern) (OpenGameArt, Liberated Pixel Cup asset set)** — genuine pixel art, tavern-themed: furniture, stoves/fireplaces, pots, brewing vessels, lighting, glassware/utensils, food props, plus sample Tiled scenes. **License: CC-BY-SA 3.0**, same commercial-use-with-share-alike-and-attribution terms as above. Important caveat stated directly on the page: preview scenes mix in tiles from *other* OpenGameArt submissions under potentially different licenses, so the bundled CREDITS file needs checking before treating the whole preview as one license. This is props/tileset material, useful for compositing a custom scene, but not a ready-made full-bleed background — it would need assembly work, and no wood-plank/tileable backdrop is included in the pack itself.
- **[Tavern Background](https://selfmaidenassets.itch.io/tavern-background/purchase) (itch.io, SelfMaidenAssets)** — a paid illustration ($2.99 minimum, pay-what-you-want above that), delivered as a 28MB zip with a "Standard License Agreement" PDF. Not confirmed as pixel art or tileable from the listing copy alone — the product page doesn't state either property, and the license terms live in a PDF not surfaced by the page itself, so this one would need the actual license document read before relying on it for commercial use.
- **[Cozy Tavern Interior Pack](https://mangoseven.itch.io/cozy-tavern-interior-pack/purchase) (itch.io, MangoSeven)** — 16×16 pixel art, free tier (7.4kB) or $1 paid tier (24kB) with optional pay-what-you-want. Genuinely cheap and genuinely pixel art, but the listing page surfaces no explicit license terms and no confirmation of a full-bleed tileable background vs. discrete interior tiles/props — would need direct inspection of the download before use, and (like most itch.io "free/PWYW" listings) commercial-use terms aren't guaranteed just because it's cheap; they'd need to be checked per-listing.
- **[Pixel Art Medieval Tavern](https://ser-alfred.itch.io/pixel-art-medieval-tavern) (itch.io, free)** — a single small PNG (143×147px) of a tavern *building exterior*, not an interior/backdrop scene, and license terms aren't stated on the page beyond a "no generative AI was used" note. Doesn't fit the brief (exterior, not a tileable full-bleed interior backdrop).
- **General wood-plank pixel textures** (not tavern-specific, closer to what's already deployed): several itch.io listings explicitly grant broad commercial rights, e.g. [Background Wood – Pixel Art Texture](https://odistudio-games.itch.io/backgroudwood) states "free to use in personal and commercial projects, no attribution required, but appreciated," and [Pixel Art Wood Planks Tileset](https://ipixl.itch.io/pixel-art-wood-planks-tileset/purchase) is pay-what-you-want. These are safe, cheap options but only replicate what the current placeholder already provides — not the fuller illustrated scene issue #82 actually wants.
- **Net finding for this route:** nothing found matches the brief exactly — a *tileable, full-bleed, illustrated pixel-art tavern scene with kettles/teapots/cups/sugar cubes* is a fairly specific composition, and asset-pack marketplaces mostly stock either (a) prop/tileset packs meant for assembly in a tile engine, not a single seamless full-bleed backdrop, or (b) non-tileable static illustration backgrounds. The closest fits (LPC Tavern for props, OpenGameArt's Tavern background for composition reference) both need either assembly work or a share-alike license decision. This route is realistic as a **starting point to assemble/collage from**, not a drop-in single-file solution — which pushes the actual assembly effort back onto the maintainer or an artist regardless.

## Recommendation

**AI image generation via a dedicated pixel-art tool (Retro Diffusion or PixelLab), with a fallback to a cheap human commission (Fiverr/itch.io) if the AI output doesn't converge on something usable within a short iteration budget.**

Rationale:

- This is explicitly a nice-to-have polish pass on an already-shippable placeholder, so the deciding factor should be **effort-to-result ratio for a solo maintainer**, not maximum achievable quality. AI generation wins there: cents per attempt and same-day iteration means a maintainer can try 10-20 compositions in an afternoon for the cost of a coffee, versus committing money and a multi-day wait to a human commission before knowing if the result fits.
- Both Retro Diffusion and PixelLab are purpose-built for genuine pixel-art constraints (grid alignment, limited palette, palette-forcing options) rather than being general models with a pixelation filter, which was the specific quality bar in the brief — this isn't the "AI slop" risk that naive Midjourney/DALL-E prompting would carry.
- The realistic weak point is composition control (getting the specific "kettles, teapots, cups, sugar cubes" arrangement to read well as a coherent full-bleed scene) and the tiling/seam pass, which the tools don't automate. Budget an extra finishing step in Aseprite/Photopea to make edges tile cleanly, mirroring whatever process produced the current wood-plank placeholder.
- The licensed-asset-pack route is not recommended as a primary path: nothing found on itch.io or OpenGameArt is a genuine drop-in match for a tileable, illustrated, prop-specific tavern scene, and the closest candidates carry a CC-BY-SA share-alike clause that deserves a deliberate decision (not a rubber stamp) given this app could carry ads/subscriptions later. It remains useful only as visual/compositional reference, or as a source of individual prop sprites (e.g. LPC Tavern's kettles/pots) to hand-place into a generated or commissioned background.
- **If AI iteration doesn't land on something good enough** (composition keeps looking generic, or hand-fixing pixel-level detail turns out to need actual pixel-art skill), the fallback is a cheap, fast human commission — Fiverr's pixel-art-background gigs start around $15-30 with days-not-weeks turnaround, which is a low-stakes way to get a professional result without the itch.io forum-vetting overhead. This keeps total worst-case spend and time modest either way.

---

**Not recommended as primary routes, with reasons:** raw Stable Diffusion + LoRA (real setup/tuning effort for no quality advantage over the dedicated tools here), and licensed asset packs as a sole source (no exact-fit listing found; share-alike licensing risk on the closest candidates; would still require assembly work that erodes the "instant download" advantage this route is supposed to offer).
